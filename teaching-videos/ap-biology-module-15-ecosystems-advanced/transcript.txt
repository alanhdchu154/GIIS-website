Quick first cycle — water. Solar energy drives the whole loop: evaporation off the ocean and transpiration from plants put vapor in the air, which condenses into clouds and falls as precipitation. On land, that water either runs off the surface back toward the sea or infiltrates down into the soil and groundwater. Keep an eye on runoff — that's how fertilizer ends up in the Gulf.

Now phosphorus — and notice how it breaks the pattern. Phosphate is freed from rock by weathering, enters soil and water, gets taken up by plants, passes to consumers, and returns to soil and sediments. The key feature: there is no atmospheric step. Phosphorus has no gas phase, so the cycle is slow, rate-limited by rock weathering. That scarcity is exactly why phosphorus is so often the limiting nutrient in freshwater and many soils.

Here's the trap the AP loves. Carbon and nitrogen both have huge atmospheric reservoirs — CO2 in the air, N2 making up most of the atmosphere — so they cycle relatively fast. Phosphorus has no gas phase at all; it crawls through rock, soil, water, and sediment. That single difference, the missing atmospheric pool, is why phosphorus is slow and why it's so often the nutrient that limits growth. Never write that phosphorus evaporates.

Now the dead zone, step by step. Fertilizer — the nitrogen and phosphorus we've been tracking — runs off farms and floods coastal water with nutrients. That triggers an enormous algal bloom. The algae die, sink, and decomposers go to work breaking them down. Here's the punchline: those decomposers consume the dissolved oxygen, the water goes hypoxic, and fish suffocate. They don't die from algal poison — they die from oxygen loss. That's the Gulf of Mexico dead zone.

Biomagnification is the AP favorite, so get the cause right. A fat-soluble toxin — DDT, mercury, or PCBs — is barely present in plankton, but the body can't break it down or excrete it. Each predator eats many contaminated prey and keeps all that toxin, so concentration climbs about tenfold per trophic step — roughly a thousandfold from producer to top predator. The toxin is retained while energy is lost, so it's most concentrated at the top. That's why DDT thinned the eagles' eggs.

Two terms students constantly swap. Bioaccumulation is buildup within a single organism over its lifetime — one fish getting more contaminated the longer it lives. Biomagnification is the increase in concentration as you move up trophic levels, so top predators carry the heaviest load. Both can happen at once, but remember: bioaccumulation is within one body over time; biomagnification is between levels, up the chain. Keep them straight and the point is yours.

Climate change hits the ocean two distinct ways, and the AP wants them apart. First, warming: greenhouse gases trap heat, and elevated sea-surface temperature is the primary cause of coral bleaching — heat-stressed corals expel the symbiotic zooxanthellae from Module Thirteen and turn white. Second, a separate chemistry problem: CO2 dissolves into seawater forming carbonic acid, lowering pH. That's ocean acidification, and its main harm is impaired calcification — corals and shellfish can't build their calcium-carbonate skeletons. Warming bleaches; acidification weakens shells.

Three more human impacts, quickly. Habitat destruction — deforestation, urbanization, agriculture — is the leading cause of biodiversity loss, full stop. Invasive species like zebra mussels, kudzu, and brown tree snakes disrupt native communities that never evolved alongside them. And overharvesting — taking faster than populations can rebound — collapsed the Atlantic cod fishery and drove megafauna extinctions. Each one chips away at the biodiversity and stability we discussed back in Module Thirteen.

Last stop — the AP productivity lab, pure logic. Pond water in three bottles. The initial bottle sets the starting-oxygen baseline. The light bottle does both photosynthesis and respiration, so its net oxygen change is NPP. The dark bottle gets no light, so only respiration runs and oxygen drops — that's respiration. Key assumption: respiration runs at the same rate in both, so we add the dark bottle's drop back to the light bottle's net. So GPP equals NPP plus respiration.

Your turn — a productivity data set. You start at eight milligrams per liter of dissolved oxygen. After a day, the light bottle reads ten, and the dark bottle reads five. Compute three things: net primary productivity from the light bottle, respiration from the dark bottle, and gross primary productivity. Remember the dark bottle gives you the respiration to add back. Pause now. Solve it. Press play when you're ready.

Here's the worked answer. Net primary productivity is the light bottle minus the start: ten minus eight equals plus two milligrams per liter. Respiration is the drop in the dark bottle: eight minus five equals three. And gross primary productivity adds respiration back: two plus three equals five. Notice GPP, five, is larger than NPP, two — it always must be, because gross is before respiration is subtracted. That's the whole logic.

Recap. Water cycle: solar-driven evaporation, transpiration, condensation, precipitation, runoff, infiltration. Phosphorus: no atmosphere, slow, often limiting — the odd one out. Eutrophication kills fish through oxygen loss during decomposition, not poison — that's the Gulf dead zone. Biomagnification concentrates toxins up the chain, hitting top predators hardest; bioaccumulation is within one body. Greenhouse gases warm; dissolved CO2 separately acidifies the ocean; both bleach coral. Habitat loss, invasives, overharvesting all cut biodiversity. And light bottle equals NPP, dark equals respiration, GPP equals their sum.

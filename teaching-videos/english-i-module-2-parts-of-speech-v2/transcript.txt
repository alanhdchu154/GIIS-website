Welcome to English One, Module Two — Parts of Speech and Grammar Foundations. Every sentence you read or write is a small team of words, each playing a role. Today you learn the eight roles, what each one does, and how to spot mistakes that confuse readers.

Read this sentence: The tired student quickly finished her essay. Every word is doing a job. Some name things, some show action, some color in detail, some glue the sentence together. A sentence is a team, and once you know the positions, you can fix any play that goes wrong.

Game plan. First, the sentence core — nouns, pronouns, verbs. Second, the modifiers — adjectives and adverbs. Third, the connectors and signals — prepositions, conjunctions, interjections. Then we hunt the trap of same word, different job, and finish with two pause-and-try sentences to lock it in.

Start with the core three. Nouns name people, places, things, or ideas — student, school, courage. Pronouns stand in for nouns so you don't repeat them — she, it, they. Verbs show action or a state of being — wrote, runs, is. Without these three, you do not have a sentence.

Modifiers paint detail onto the core. Adjectives describe nouns — a tired student, a clever idea, the loud bell. Adverbs describe verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs, and often end in l-y — wrote quickly, very tired, almost finished. The same word can shift roles, so always judge by what it modifies.

The last three are the glue. Prepositions show relationships — on the desk, before class, after lunch. Conjunctions link words or clauses — and, but, because, although. Interjections inject feeling — wow, ouch, hey. Without connectors, sentences would just be a pile of disconnected nouns and verbs.

Here is the trap that costs students the most points. A word's part of speech depends on the job, not the spelling. Run is a verb in I run home, but a noun in we won the run. Light is an adjective in a light backpack, a noun in the light is on, and a verb in light the candle. Always ask what this word is doing in this sentence.

Your turn. Label every word in this sentence: The clever fox quickly jumped over the lazy dog. Nine words, nine labels. Pause now. Solve it. Press play when you are ready.

Time. The — article, a kind of adjective. Clever — adjective. Fox — noun. Quickly — adverb, modifying jumped. Jumped — verb. Over — preposition. The — article again. Lazy — adjective. Dog — noun. Notice how clever describes fox, while quickly describes jumped. That is how you tell adjectives from adverbs.

Now use parts of speech as a revision tool. Take the sentence: The thing was good. Two weak words — thing is an empty noun, and good is a tired adjective. Swap them for specific ones. The argument was persuasive. Same sentence shape, real meaning. Labels are not just a quiz; they are a way to revise.

Pause and try. Choose the correct word: She speaks clear or clearly during the debate. One is an adjective, one is an adverb. Pause now. Pick one and explain why. Press play when ready.

Time. The verb is speaks. To describe a verb, you need an adverb, not an adjective. Clear would describe a noun — a clear voice. Clearly describes the verb — speaks clearly. So the answer is clearly. Quick check: does the word modify a noun or a verb.

Recap. Eight parts of speech, each with a job. The core — nouns, pronouns, verbs. The modifiers — adjectives, adverbs. The connectors and signals — prepositions, conjunctions, interjections. The same word can change role, so judge by job. Adjectives modify nouns. Adverbs modify verbs and other modifiers.

Welcome back to AP Biology. This is Module Ten: Natural Selection and Evolution. Today's goal is simple: by the end, you should be able to explain how a population changes without imagining that individual organisms transform on purpose.

Start with the peppered moth story. The common wrong idea is that moths saw soot, needed to survive, and turned darker. That is not evolution. Light moths stayed light. Dark moths stayed dark. The population changed because the environment changed which variants survived long enough to reproduce.

Here is our roadmap. First, natural selection needs four ingredients. Second, Hardy-Weinberg gives us a baseline for no evolution. Third, five mechanisms break that baseline. Finally, we will practice the exact AP trap: starting from the recessive phenotype.

Natural selection is not mysterious. It needs variation, heritability, overproduction, and differential reproductive success. If a trait varies, some of that variation is inherited, more offspring are born than can survive, and some variants leave more offspring, then the population shifts over generations.

Pause mentally on this sentence: selection acts on phenotypes, but evolution is measured in populations. A bodybuilder's large muscles may help in a contest, but that acquired muscle is not passed to children. Heritable variation is the key.

Fitness does not mean strong, fast, or impressive. In biology, fitness means relative reproductive success. A tiny mouse with twelve reproducing grandchildren has higher fitness than a huge mouse with two. Fitness is always measured in a specific environment.

Hardy-Weinberg is our no-evolution model. If allele A has frequency p and allele a has frequency q, then p plus q equals one. Genotypes follow p squared, two p q, and q squared. The point is not that nature obeys this perfectly. The point is that deviations tell us something changed.

Pause and try this. In a population of four hundred mice, sixty-four are white. White is the homozygous recessive aa phenotype. Assuming Hardy-Weinberg, what is the frequency of the dominant A allele? Pause now. Solve it before you continue.

The recessive phenotype gives you q squared. Sixty-four divided by four hundred is point one six. The square root of point one six is point four, so q equals point four. Since p plus q equals one, p equals point six. The AP trick is to start from q squared, not from p.

Five mechanisms can move a population away from Hardy-Weinberg. Mutation creates new alleles. Gene flow moves alleles between populations. Genetic drift is random sampling, strongest in small populations. Natural selection is non-random with respect to fitness. Non-random mating reshuffles genotypes.

Do not confuse drift and selection. Selection is biased by reproductive success. Drift is chance. In a bottleneck, survivors may not be better adapted; they may simply be the ones that happened to live. A helpful allele can disappear, and a harmful allele can become common.

Evolution is supported by many independent lines of evidence: fossils, biogeography, homologous structures, vestigial structures, developmental patterns, DNA sequences, and direct observation such as antibiotic resistance. The strongest answer is rarely one fact. It is the agreement across many kinds of evidence.

Lock these in. Individuals do not evolve; populations do. Selection acts on phenotypes, but only heritable variation changes future generations. Hardy-Weinberg is a baseline. Drift is random, selection is not. And evolution has no goal, no ladder, and no promise of perfection.

Welcome back. Algebra One, Module Eight — graphing linear functions. Today we turn an equation into a picture you can read at a glance. By the end you can graph a line three different ways and tell the slope, the x-intercept, and the y-intercept from any one of them.

Your phone plan charges fifteen dollars a month plus ten cents per text. Plot total cost against number of texts. The graph is a straight line — and every point on that line answers a real question. How much for one hundred texts? Look up at the line. That is what graphing a linear equation does.

Game plan. First, the table-of-values method — plug in x, compute y, plot the pairs. Second, the intercepts method — find where the line hits each axis. Third, slope-intercept form — y equals m x plus b reads off the slope and the y-intercept at a glance.

The big idea. The graph of a linear equation is the set of every x and y pair that makes the equation true. Plot enough true pairs and a clean straight line appears. Two points are enough to draw the line, but three or four catch arithmetic mistakes before you connect them.

Method one — table of values. Take y equals two x plus one. Pick four easy x values — negative one, zero, one, and two. Plug each into the rule. You get y values of negative one, one, three, and five. Plot the four pairs and connect them. The line passes right through every point.

Method two — intercepts. The x-intercept is where the line crosses the x-axis, so set y to zero. The y-intercept is where it crosses the y-axis, so set x to zero. Take three x plus two y equals six. Setting y to zero gives x equals two. Setting x to zero gives y equals three. Two intercepts, one line.

Your turn. Graph y equals negative x plus three using a table of values. Use the x values negative one, zero, one, and two. Find the y for each, write the pairs, and plot them. Pause now. Solve it. Press play when you are ready.

Time. The rule is y equals negative x plus three. At x equals negative one, y equals four. At x equals zero, y equals three. At x equals one, y equals two. At x equals two, y equals one. Plot the four pairs and connect them. The line slopes downhill — every one step right drops y by one — which matches the negative coefficient on x.

Method three — slope-intercept form. Any equation written as y equals m x plus b tells you two things instantly. The b is the y-intercept — the point zero comma b. The m is the slope — rise over run. Plot b on the y-axis, then count m to find a second point. Two points, draw the line.

The trap. Students mix up x-intercept and y-intercept. To find an x-intercept you set y to zero, because every point on the x-axis has y equal to zero. To find a y-intercept you set x to zero. Same idea, opposite axis. Swap them and your line drifts to the wrong corner of the graph.

Your turn. The equation is y equals two x minus four. Use slope-intercept form. Identify the slope and the y-intercept. Then plot the y-intercept and use the slope to land a second point. Pause now. Solve it. Press play when you are ready.

Time. Compare y equals two x minus four to y equals m x plus b. The slope m is two. The y-intercept b is negative four. Plot the point zero comma negative four on the y-axis. From there, rise two and run one to land at one comma negative two. Two points — draw the line. Positive slope, so it tilts uphill.

How the assignment uses this. You will graph eight linear equations using Desmos or pencil and graph paper. For each one, write down the x-intercept, the y-intercept, and label the slope as positive, negative, zero, or undefined. Show the table or the intercept work — not just the picture.

Recap. The graph of a linear equation is every x and y pair that makes the equation true, and those pairs always line up. Three ways in — a table of values, the two intercepts, or slope-intercept form. Positive slope tilts up, negative tilts down, zero is flat, vertical is undefined.

Welcome back. Algebra One, Module Eleven — systems of linear equations. Today we stop solving one equation in isolation. We solve two equations at once, and find the point that satisfies both at the same time.

Two gyms. Plan A — thirty dollars a month plus five dollars per visit. Plan B — ten dollars per visit, no monthly fee. When do they cost the same? That single question is a system — two cost lines, one shared point. By the end of this lesson you will solve it three different ways.

Game plan. Three methods — graphing, substitution, and elimination. Same system. Three ways to find the same ordered pair. We will also learn what it means when a system has no solution, or infinitely many.

The big idea. A system of equations is two equations sharing one unknown x and one unknown y. A solution is an ordered pair that makes both equations true at the same time. Graphically, that is exactly the point where the two lines cross.

Method one — graphing. Take y equals two x plus one and y equals negative x plus seven. Plot each line. They cross at the point two comma five. Check by substitution. Two times two plus one is five. Negative two plus seven is also five. Both equations agree, so two comma five is the solution.

Method two — substitution. Take y equals two x plus one and three x plus y equals sixteen. The first equation already says what y equals, so substitute that expression into the second. Three x plus the quantity two x plus one equals sixteen. Combine. Five x plus one equals sixteen. Five x equals fifteen. x equals three, so y equals seven. Solution — three comma seven.

Time. y equals x plus two, so substitute that into the second equation. Two x plus the quantity x plus two equals eight. Combine like terms. Three x plus two equals eight. Subtract two. Three x equals six. x equals two. Now y equals two plus two, so y equals four. Solution — two comma four. Check — two times two plus four is eight. It works.

Method three — elimination. Take two x plus three y equals twelve and four x minus three y equals six. The y-terms are opposites, so add the equations straight down. The y-terms cancel. Six x equals eighteen. x equals three. Substitute back. Two times three plus three y equals twelve gives three y equals six, so y equals two. Solution — three comma two.

Three possibilities. If the lines have different slopes, they cross once — one solution. If the slopes are equal but the y-intercepts differ, the lines are parallel — no solution. If both equations describe the same line, every point on that line works — infinitely many solutions. In algebra, no solution shows up as a false statement like five equals nine. Infinite shows up as a true statement like zero equals zero.

The trap. When you multiply an equation to set up elimination, multiply every term, not just the one you want to cancel. If the equation is two x plus three y equals twelve and you multiply by two, every term doubles — four x plus six y equals twenty-four. A common mistake is doubling only the y-term and leaving the constant unchanged, which silently changes the system.

How the assignment uses this. You will solve nine systems — three by graphing, three by substitution, three by elimination. Then you will write your own word problem that a system can model, and solve it. Label each method clearly. Give every answer as an ordered pair. Verify at least three by substituting back into both equations.

Recap. Three methods — graphing for visual systems, substitution when one equation is already solved, elimination when matching coefficients line up. One solution means the lines cross. No solution means parallel. Infinite means same line. Multiply every term when scaling, and always check the ordered pair in both equations.

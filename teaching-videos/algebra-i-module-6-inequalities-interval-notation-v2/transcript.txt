Welcome back. Algebra One, Module Six — Inequalities and Interval Notation. Same balance moves you already know, plus one new rule and a new way to write the answer. By the end you can solve, graph on a number line, and translate the solution into interval notation.

You have fifty dollars to spend on snacks. Each snack costs four dollars. How many can you buy? Not exactly fifty — at most fifty. The answer is not a single number — it is a whole range. That is what an inequality is. Four x is less than or equal to fifty, and x lives somewhere on a number line.

Game plan. First, solve a linear inequality using the same balance moves you already know. Second, the one new rule — flip the inequality sign when you multiply or divide both sides by a negative. Third, write the solution three ways — number line, interval notation, and set-builder.

The core idea. An inequality asks for every number that makes a comparison true — less than, greater than, less than or equal, greater than or equal. The solution is not one number — it is an interval on the number line. Open circle means the endpoint is not included. Closed dot means it is.

The killer mistake. Multiplying or dividing both sides by a negative without flipping the inequality. Negative two x less than ten. If you divide by negative two and keep the sign, you get x less than negative five — wrong. Flip the sign — x greater than negative five. Test with x equals zero — it works. Always flip when the multiplier is negative.

Solve three x plus five less than fourteen. Subtract five from both sides — three x less than nine. Divide both sides by positive three. No flip — the divisor is positive. x less than three. Graph it — open circle at three, shading left. Interval — negative infinity, three — both ends in round brackets, because neither endpoint is included.

Solve negative two x plus seven greater than or equal to one. Subtract seven from both sides — negative two x greater than or equal to negative six. Divide both sides by negative two — and flip the sign. x less than or equal to three. Graph it — closed dot at three, shading left. Interval — negative infinity, three — round bracket on the left, square bracket on the right, because three is included.

Time. Add three to both sides — four x greater than or equal to twenty. Divide both sides by positive four — no flip needed. x greater than or equal to five. Graph — closed dot at five, shading right. Interval — square bracket five, comma, infinity, round bracket. Infinity never gets a square bracket — you can never reach it.

Now the translation. Three ways to say the same solution. Number line — picture. Interval notation — two endpoints in brackets. Set-builder — the rule in plain math. Round bracket means open — endpoint excluded — same as an open circle. Square bracket means closed — endpoint included — same as a closed dot. Infinity always uses a round bracket.

Your turn. Look at this graph — closed dot at negative two, shading to the right. Write the solution in interval notation. Then write it in set-builder notation. Pause now. Translate it. Press play when you are ready.

Time. Closed dot at negative two means negative two is included. Shading to the right means every number greater than negative two also works. Interval — square bracket negative two, comma, infinity, round bracket. Set-builder — the set of all x such that x is greater than or equal to negative two. Same solution, three forms.

Recap. Same balance moves as an equation, with one rule on top — flip the sign when you multiply or divide both sides by a negative. Solutions live on the number line as intervals. Round bracket means open, square bracket means closed, and infinity always uses a round bracket.

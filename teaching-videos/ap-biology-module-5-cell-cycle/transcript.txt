Welcome back to AP Biology — Module Five, Cell Cycle and Division. This finishes College Board Unit Four, which lands on roughly ten to fifteen percent of the AP exam. We're going to look at how cells decide to divide, the choreography of mitosis, and what happens when that decision system breaks — cancer.

Right now, while you sit there, your body is producing roughly three point eight million new cells every second. Each one of them just made a decision — divide, or don't. That decision happens at a checkpoint, controlled by a handful of proteins. When even one of those proteins breaks, the cell divides when it shouldn't. That is what cancer is. So today's lesson is the most important regulatory system you have.

Three things to lock in. First — the cell cycle itself: G1, S, G2, and M. Second — mitosis and cytokinesis, the five phases and how animal and plant cells split differently. Third — how the cycle is regulated by cyclins, CDKs, and checkpoints, and what cancer looks like when those brakes fail.

Here is the whole cycle. Interphase is the long part — G1 for growth, S for DNA synthesis, G2 for more growth and getting ready for mitosis. Then M phase — mitosis splits the nucleus, cytokinesis splits the cytoplasm. Cells that finish their job and stop dividing exit at G1 into G0 — that is where most of your neurons and mature muscle cells live. They are alive and working, just not dividing.

Zoom into interphase. In G1, the cell grows, makes proteins, and duplicates its organelles — but the DNA is still one copy per chromosome. In S phase, DNA replication happens — every chromosome gets copied, so now each chromosome has two identical sister chromatids joined at the centromere. In G2, more growth, and the cell builds the centrosomes and microtubules it will need for mitosis. Remember the order — G1, S, G2 — and that DNA replication happens in S, before mitosis ever starts.

Mitosis is traditionally taught in four phases — prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase — the classic PMAT mnemonic. That's the framing the AP College Board uses too. Many treatments go one level deeper by splitting prometaphase out of late prophase, giving five distinct phases. Either way, what matters is the events themselves, and we'll walk through them next. A trick for the order: people meet at the middle, then apart they go. Prophase, prometaphase, metaphase — apart in anaphase — telophase wraps it up.

What actually happens in each phase. Prophase — chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes, the nucleolus disappears, and the spindle starts forming from the centrosomes. Prometaphase — the nuclear envelope breaks down and spindle microtubules attach to chromosomes at kinetochores. Metaphase — chromosomes line up single-file at the metaphase plate, the cell's equator. Anaphase — separase cleaves cohesin, sister chromatids separate and get pulled to opposite poles. Telophase — nuclear envelopes reform around each set, chromosomes decondense, and the spindle disassembles.

Cytokinesis splits the cytoplasm, and animal cells and plant cells do it completely differently. Animal cells form a cleavage furrow — an actin and myosin contractile ring pinches the cell in two from the outside in, like a drawstring. Plant cells can't pinch because they have a rigid cell wall, so they build a new wall from the middle outward — vesicles from the Golgi fuse to form a cell plate, which becomes the new cell wall separating the two daughters.

Three checkpoints regulate the cycle. The G1 checkpoint — also called the restriction point — is the most important. The cell asks: is my DNA intact, am I big enough, and do I have growth signals from outside? If yes, commit to dividing. If no, exit to G0. The G2 checkpoint verifies DNA replication completed correctly with no damage. The M-phase spindle checkpoint verifies every chromosome's kinetochore is attached to the spindle before letting anaphase start — otherwise you'd split chromosomes unevenly.

Try this. A cell with damaged DNA enters G1. What single protein detects the damage at the G1 checkpoint, and what are the two possible outcomes that protein triggers? Pause now. Think it through. Press play when you're ready.

Answer. The protein is p53 — nicknamed the guardian of the genome. When p53 detects DNA damage at the G1 checkpoint, it does one of two things. First option — it halts the cell cycle and recruits DNA repair machinery to try to fix the damage, then lets the cell resume. Second option — if the damage is too severe to repair, p53 triggers apoptosis, programmed cell death. That second job is exactly why p53 is mutated in over half of all human cancers — without it, broken cells keep dividing.

How do checkpoints actually work at the molecular level? Two protein families: cyclins and cyclin-dependent kinases, or CDKs. CDK concentrations stay roughly constant, but they're inactive on their own. Cyclin concentrations rise and fall through the cycle — when a specific cyclin builds up high enough, it binds to its partner CDK and activates it. The active complex then phosphorylates target proteins that push the cell into the next phase. The most famous example — MPF, M-phase Promoting Factor — is cyclin B bound to CDK1, and it is what triggers entry into mitosis.

Now cancer. Two categories of genes go wrong. Proto-oncogenes are normal genes that promote cell division — Ras and Myc are classic examples. When mutated to oncogenes, they gain function and over-promote division. These are dominant at the cell level — one mutated copy is enough to push the cell toward dividing. Tumor suppressor genes do the opposite — they normally restrain division or trigger apoptosis. p53 and Rb are the famous ones. These are loss-of-function — and they are recessive at the cell level, meaning both alleles need to be knocked out before the brakes fail. Cancer almost always requires multiple mutations stacking up.

Common trap — students mix up oncogenes and tumor suppressors. Side by side. Oncogenes — gain of function, gas pedal stuck down, one bad copy is enough, dominant at the cell level. Tumor suppressors — loss of function, brakes cut, both copies must be knocked out, recessive at the cell level. Same outcome — uncontrolled division — but completely opposite mechanisms. On the AP exam, if a question says 'one mutation caused over-division,' it's an oncogene. If it says 'both alleles had to be lost,' it's a tumor suppressor.

Recap. The cycle goes G1, S, G2, M — with G0 as the exit ramp. DNA replicates in S, giving each chromosome two sister chromatids. Mitosis has five phases — prophase, prometaphase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase — and sister chromatids separate in anaphase when separase cuts cohesin. Cytokinesis: animal uses a cleavage furrow, plant builds a cell plate. Cyclins and CDKs regulate the checkpoints; p53 guards the G1 checkpoint. Cancer is loss of control — oncogenes gain function, tumor suppressors lose function.

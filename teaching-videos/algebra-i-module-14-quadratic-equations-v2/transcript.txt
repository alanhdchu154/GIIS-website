Welcome back. Algebra One, Module Fourteen — quadratic equations. Last lesson you factored polynomials. Today you turn that factoring into actual answers, and you learn three different ways to solve when factoring alone is not enough.

Here is an equation — x squared plus five x plus six equals zero. It looks like the trinomials you just factored, but now there is an equals zero on the right. That single equals sign is the doorway between a polynomial and a quadratic equation, and the zero is the key that unlocks the solutions.

Game plan. First, the zero-product property that turns a factored quadratic into two simple equations. Second, three solving methods — factoring, completing the square, and the quadratic formula. Third, the discriminant, a single number that tells you whether to expect zero, one, or two real solutions before you solve.

Standard form. A quadratic equation is a x squared plus b x plus c equals zero, where a is not zero. The solutions are the x-values that make the left side equal zero. The zero-product property says — if two factors multiply to zero, at least one of them is zero. That is the bridge from factoring to solving.

Method one — factoring. Step one, get everything on one side so the right is zero. Step two, factor the trinomial using the skills from Module Thirteen. Step three, set each factor equal to zero and solve. For x squared plus five x plus six equals zero, the factored form is open paren x plus two close paren times open paren x plus three close paren equals zero. So x equals negative two or x equals negative three.

Method two — completing the square. When factoring is hard, force a perfect square trinomial. Move the constant to the other side. Take half of b, square it, and add it to both sides. The left collapses into a binomial squared, and you finish by taking the square root of both sides. This method is the proof behind the quadratic formula — and it always works.

Method three — the quadratic formula. For any a x squared plus b x plus c equals zero, x equals negative b plus or minus the square root of b squared minus four a c, all divided by two a. Memorize it cold. Identify a, b, and c carefully — signs and all — then plug in and simplify. This formula is the safety net when nothing factors nicely.

Your turn. Solve x squared minus seven x plus ten equals zero by factoring. Find two numbers that multiply to ten and add to negative seven, then use the zero-product property. Pause now. Solve it. Press play when you are ready.

Time. Factor pairs of ten — one and ten, two and five. We need a sum of negative seven, so both numbers are negative. Negative two plus negative five is negative seven. So the factored form is open paren x minus two close paren times open paren x minus five close paren equals zero. By the zero-product property, x equals two or x equals five. Check by substituting each value back — both should give zero.

The discriminant. Look inside the quadratic formula at b squared minus four a c. That number alone tells you how many real solutions to expect. Positive — two real solutions. Zero — one repeated real solution. Negative — no real solutions. Compute the discriminant first, and you know what kind of answer is coming before you finish.

The classic trap. Students try to factor before moving every term to one side, or they solve x squared plus five x equals six by factoring the left and setting it equal to six. The zero-product property only works when the product equals zero. Always rearrange to standard form a x squared plus b x plus c equals zero first.

Your turn. Use the quadratic formula to solve two x squared plus four x minus six equals zero. Identify a, b, and c — then plug into negative b plus or minus square root of b squared minus four a c, over two a. Pause now. Solve it. Press play when you are ready.

Time. Here a equals two, b equals four, c equals negative six. The discriminant is four squared minus four times two times negative six, which is sixteen plus forty eight equals sixty four. Square root of sixty four is eight. So x equals negative four plus or minus eight, all over four. That gives x equals one or x equals negative three. Two real solutions, exactly as the positive discriminant predicted.

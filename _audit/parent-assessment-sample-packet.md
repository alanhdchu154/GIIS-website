Module 1: Reading Comprehension Strategies

Assignment type: Research / Evidence Report

Prompt: Read one short informational article from a free news, museum, library, or government education site and one short public-domain story from a source such as Project Gutenberg or a teacher-provided packet. For each: (1) write a one-paragraph summary in your own words, (2) identify the main idea and three supporting details, (3) make two inferences supported with text evidence, and (4) identify the text structure used.

Rubric focus: Source credibility, evidence use, reasoning, citations or links, and clarity.

### Assessment Samples

Quiz sample:

Question: What is the main idea of a passage?
Options: The first sentence of the passage / The most important point the author wants to convey / A specific detail that supports the author's argument / A quotation from an expert
Answer: The most important point the author wants to convey
Explanation: The main idea is the central, overarching point of the passage — what the entire text is mostly about.

Midterm sample:

Question: Which strategy involves identifying what a text is mostly about, stated in one or two sentences?
Options: Inference / Summarizing / Identifying main idea / Analyzing text structure
Answer: Identifying main idea
Explanation: The main idea is the central point of a passage, stated concisely. Summarizing covers the whole passage; inference draws conclusions not stated directly.

Final sample:

Question: Explain the difference between a metaphor and a simile. Give one original example of each.
Answer: A simile compares two things using 'like' or 'as' (e.g., 'Her voice was like music'). A metaphor states directly that one thing IS another without using 'like' or 'as' (e.g., 'Her voice was music to my ears').
Explanation: Both are figures of speech that make comparisons, but similes use 'like' or 'as' while metaphors make a direct identification between two unlike things.

Module 1: Literary Analysis Fundamentals

Assignment type: Research / Evidence Report

Prompt: Read a teacher-provided public-domain excerpt or a short open text selected from the linked literature resources. Identify: (1) the central theme and two pieces of textual evidence supporting it, (2) one symbol and explain what it represents, (3) one recurring motif and its significance, (4) whether the text is allegorical and, if so, what real-world concept the story represents, and (5) the author's purpose. Expected evidence: Student submits sources, evidence notes, and a written explanation using course vocabulary.

Rubric focus: Source credibility, evidence use, reasoning, citations or links, and clarity.

### Assessment Samples

Quiz sample:

Question: Which of the following best describes a theme in a literary work?
Options: A summary of the plot from beginning to end / The name of the main character / A universal message or insight about human experience conveyed by the text / The physical setting of the story
Answer: A universal message or insight about human experience conveyed by the text
Explanation: Theme is the central idea or message — a statement about life, human nature, or society that the text communicates through its characters, conflicts, and events.

Midterm sample:

Question: A symbol in a literary text is best described as:
Options: A character's name that has a hidden meaning / An object, person, or event that represents something beyond its literal meaning / A comparison using 'like' or 'as' / The resolution of a conflict
Answer: An object, person, or event that represents something beyond its literal meaning
Explanation: A symbol carries both a literal identity and a deeper representational meaning — for example, a broken mirror might symbolize shattered dreams.

Final sample:

Question: Explain the difference between tone and mood. Then, using a text you have studied this year, give one example of each and explain what specific language choices create them.
Answer: Tone is the author's attitude toward the subject (e.g., ironic, mournful, celebratory). Mood is the emotional atmosphere the reader experiences. Example from 'The Raven': Poe's tone is mournful and obsessive — created by diction choices like 'nevermore,' 'lost Lenore,' and 'bleak December.' The mood is one of dread and despair, created by the dark imagery of a raven in a dark room and repetitive refrain that makes the reader feel trapped alongside the narrator.
Explanation: Tone = writer's attitude (craft choice); Mood = reader's emotional response (effect). Both are produced primarily through diction, imagery, and syntax.
